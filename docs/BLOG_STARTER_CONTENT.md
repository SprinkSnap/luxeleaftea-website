| **Excerpt** | Learn the right grams, water temperature, and steep times for premium loose leaf tea — green, oolong, pu-erh, and black. |

### Body (paste into rich text editor)

```html
<p>Premium loose leaf tea rewards a little care. With the right leaf amount, water temperature, and timing, whole leaves unfurl into a clearer, more aromatic cup than any tea bag.</p>

<h2>How much leaf to use</h2>
<p>For most premium loose leaf teas, use <strong>3 grams of leaf per 150ml of water</strong> (about one heaping teaspoon for a standard mug). Oolongs and pu-erh often taste best with slightly more leaf — try 4g for a richer cup.</p>

<h2>Water temperature by tea type</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Green tea:</strong> 175–185°F (80–85°C) — never boiling</li>
  <li><strong>Oolong:</strong> 195–205°F (90–96°C)</li>
  <li><strong>Black tea:</strong> 200–212°F (93–100°C)</li>
  <li><strong>Pu-erh:</strong> 205–212°F (96–100°C), with a quick rinse first</li>
</ul>

<h2>Steep times</h2>
<p>Start with <strong>1–2 minutes</strong> for green tea, <strong>2–3 minutes</strong> for oolong, and <strong>3–4 minutes</strong> for black tea. Pu-erh often starts with a 20–30 second rinse, then 2–3 minutes on the first real infusion.</p>

<h2>Re-steeping</h2>
<p>Quality whole-leaf tea can be re-steeped multiple times. Greens often yield 3–4 infusions; oolongs and pu-erh can offer 4–8. Add 30–60 seconds per subsequent steep.</p>

<h2>Ready to brew?</h2>

```

### Body

```html
<p>Oolong sits between green and black tea — partially oxidized, layered, and often re-steepable. If you are new to premium loose leaf tea, oolong is one of the most rewarding places to start.</p>

<h2>Why oolong works for beginners</h2>
<p>Good oolongs are <strong>forgiving</strong>: they stay smooth across a range of temperatures, release aroma quickly, and improve over multiple steeps. Rolled leaves slowly unfurl, so each infusion reveals something new.</p>

<h2>Flavors to look for</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Floral &amp; creamy:</strong> Tieguanyin-style oolongs — orchid, honey, soft finish</li>
  <li><strong>Toasted &amp; layered:</strong> Yan cha / roasted styles — grain, cocoa, mineral depth</li>
  <li><strong>Light &amp; sweet:</strong> High-mountain oolongs — fresh, airy, gently sweet</li>
</ul>

<h2>Simple brewing method</h2>
<p>Use <strong>4g leaf per 150ml</strong> at <strong>195–205°F</strong>. Steep 2–3 minutes the first time, then re-steep 4–6 times. No special equipment required — a mug with an infuser works perfectly.</p>
```

### Body

```html
<p>Tea bags are convenient, but premium loose leaf tea delivers a different experience — more aroma, more clarity, and more cups from the same leaves.</p>

<h2>Whole leaf vs fannings</h2>
<p>Most tea bags use <strong>fannings or dust</strong> — small particles that release flavor fast but fade quickly. Premium loose leaf uses whole or large leaves that unfurl slowly, releasing layered flavor across multiple steeps.</p>

<h2>Flavor and aroma</h2>
<p>Whole leaves preserve essential oils and structure. The result is a cup with <strong>clearer aroma</strong>, less bitterness when brewed correctly, and a finish that lingers instead of flattening after one steep.</p>

<h2>Re-steeping saves money</h2>
<p>A single serving of quality loose leaf often yields <strong>3–8 infusions</strong>. When you divide cost per cup across steeps, premium loose leaf is often comparable to — or better than — specialty bagged tea.</p>

<h2>Origin and transparency</h2>
<p>Loose leaf sellers can label by garden, region, and harvest. You know what you are drinking — and that matters when you care about taste and quality.</p>
```
